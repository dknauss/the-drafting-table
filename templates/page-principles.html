Design Philosophy

Sheet P-001 / Foundational

Guiding Principles

The convictions that shape every line
we draw and every joint we specify

Architecture, at its most essential, is the art of making shelter meaningful. Not merely functional, not merely beautiful, but deeply connected to the life it contains and the land upon which it stands. These principles are not rules imposed from the outside; they emerge from decades of observation, from sitting on hilltops at dawn, from watching how rain moves across a limestone wall, from listening to the way a family actually lives within their rooms.

I

Truth to Materials

Let wood be wood and stone be stone. Every material has an inherent character, a grain, a weight, a way of meeting light. The architect's duty is to honor these qualities rather than disguise them. A concrete wall should show the grain of its formwork. A timber beam should reveal its species and its age. When we paint over natural stone or wrap steel in plastic cladding, we commit a kind of architectural dishonesty that the building's inhabitants will feel, even if they cannot name it.

We source materials locally wherever possible. The limestone in our foundations comes from quarries within fifty miles of the site. The oak in our cabinetry is milled from regional forests. This is not merely sustainability; it is an act of belonging. A house built from the earth beneath it carries a rootedness that no imported material can replicate.

II

Organic Integration

A building should appear to grow from its site as naturally as a tree from the soil. The roofline should trace the contour of the hill. The garden wall should dissolve into meadow. Architecture and landscape are one continuous thought. We spend more time studying the land than drawing upon it. Before any scheme takes shape, we map the movement of shadows across the site through every season. We document the prevailing winds, the drainage patterns, the location of every significant tree.

The result is a building that participates in its ecology rather than dominating it. Roof overhangs are calibrated to admit winter sun while shading summer windows. Foundation walls step with the grade rather than imposing a level platform. Native grasses are allowed to grow to the threshold. The boundary between inside and outside becomes a conversation, not a confrontation.

III

The Destruction of the Box

Rooms need not be cages. Through the elimination of the corner post, the cantilevered roof, and the ribbon window, interior space flows freely outward, dissolving the boundary between shelter and sky. We design plans that breathe. Living spaces open to dining areas without walls. Hallways become galleries. Bedrooms extend to private terraces through walls of glass.

This openness is not achieved through mere absence of partition. It requires structural ingenuity: the cantilever that frees the corner from its post, the hidden beam that allows the wall to become entirely glass, the carefully placed screen that suggests enclosure without enforcing it. The result is architecture that feels generous, expansive, and deeply connected to the world beyond its walls.

IV

The Craft of the Detail

Architecture lives or dies in the details. The way a window meets a wall, the depth of a reveal, the alignment of a mortar joint with the shelf above it. These are the moments where care becomes visible, where the difference between competent building and true architecture reveals itself. We draw every detail at full scale before it is built. The junction of copper flashing and limestone cap. The shadow gap between plaster ceiling and oak trim. The brass hardware that latches a garden door.

This obsession with the particular is not perfectionism for its own sake. It is the understanding that the people who inhabit these spaces will touch these details every day. They will rest their hands on the window ledge, push open the door, lean against the wall. Each detail is a conversation between the architect and the inhabitant, conducted through the silent language of craft.

Less is only more where more is no good. We seek the appropriate, the essential, the precisely enough.

Studio notes, Winter 2023
